BTA Rwanda

Medical Equipment Repair Lesson

Reports in multiple regions of the developing world suggest that nearly 95% of medical equipment is either being used inefficiently, or is completely unusable. Even when repairs are simple, manufacturer provided service plans are out of the financial reach of isolated hospitals and clinics.

  • Visual Design
  • User Experience
  • Illustration

Duke University’s biomedical engineering team examined the situation, and discovered that a large percentage of the repairs could be done with a low skill level. They wrote manuals to explain equipment repair procedures, but had difficulties with the language barrier. This lead them to create to reach out to design schools for visual approaches–the University of Cincinnati was the first to accept.

Biomedical Technician Assistant (BTA) lessons are divided into specific knowledge domains—1 plumbing, 2 mechanical, electrical, power supply, and motors—with difficulty levels assigned to each. My studio illustrated and revised copy for the first updated manuals to be tested in Rwanda. Each team chose one lesson to learn, simplify, and develop carefully considered diagrams for.

My team chose a lesson focusing on repairing a Blood Pressure Apparatus, and began by creating a procedural narrative by acting out and photo documenting the method described in the written manual. Each team member then individually explored illustration techniques. While choosing a medium for presenting the lesson best I considered sustainability and portability. After eliminating ePub readers as an option due to battery life, I chose to print the manuals locally as 8-fold posters (5 × 4.5 inches when folded) that allowed trainees to choose to view the process as a whole or granularly.

Today

Since its introduction, the BTA curriculum offered in Rwanda and Ghana. In Rwanda, this evidence-based curriculum has been shown to reduce out of service equipment by up to 40%.
